# Energy: Starting State and Nuclear Acceleration Pathway

**Priority #1 for AwesomeAustralia.** Energy abundance is the master resource. Cheap, reliable, high-density power collapses many other scarcities (housing construction costs, industry, desalination, AI compute, food production via controlled environments). This document establishes the baseline and outlines the fastest credible path to nuclear as a firm, high-capacity-factor source.

Data as of mid-2026.

### Installed Capacity (approximate, mid-2026 Open Electricity / AEMO-linked data)
Total system capacity ~**115 GW**.

- Solar (rooftop): ~26.9 GW
- Solar (utility): ~12.1 GW
- Wind: ~15.6 GW
- Hydro: ~8.8 GW
- Battery storage: ~11.0 GW (rapid growth)
- Coal (black + brown): ~23.4 GW
- Gas (various OCGT/CCGT/steam): ~15–16 GW
- Other (distillate, bio, etc.): smaller

**Key characteristics**: High and rising variable renewable penetration, strong rooftop solar, declining but still critical coal baseload, rapidly expanding battery storage, significant regional differences (hydro-heavy TAS, gas-heavy WA/NT, coal-heavy east coast).

Coal plants are aging with planned retirements. Reliability increasingly depends on gas peakers, hydro, batteries, and interconnections.

## Ongoing and Major Projects (Examples, mid-2026)

- **Snowy 2.0** (pumped hydro): 2,200 MW. Under construction, significant delays and cost overruns.
- **Project EnergyConnect**: Major interstate transmission (SA–NSW). Energised after delays; Transgrid portion cost escalated from ~$2.1B to ~$3.6–3.8B.
- **Battery Energy Storage Systems (BESS)**: Explosive growth. Examples: Liddell Battery (500 MW / 2-hour, ~$750M, commissioning 2026). Multiple GW-scale projects under Capacity Investment Scheme tenders (e.g., 4.2 GW / 16.1 GWh in one recent tender).
- Large Renewable Energy Zones (REZs) and associated transmission.
- Various utility solar, wind, and hybrid projects in pipeline (thousands of MW).
- Hydrogen/ammonia pilots and green industrial projects (smaller scale).
- Transmission and storage are the current focus of government support; firm low-emissions capacity beyond existing hydro/gas remains limited.

Costs for recent large batteries are in the hundreds of millions to low billions depending on size and duration. Transmission projects frequently experience 50–100%+ overruns.

## Nuclear Regulatory Barriers — What Gets Deleted

Civil nuclear power is currently prohibited.

**Primary federal barriers**:
1. **Australian Radiation Protection and Nuclear Safety Act 1998 (ARPANS Act), Section 10**: Explicitly prohibits the construction or operation of:
   - Nuclear fuel fabrication plant
   - Nuclear power plant
   - Enrichment plant
   - Reprocessing facility
   (Naval nuclear propulsion for conventionally-armed submarines is exempted for AUKUS.)

2. **Environment Protection and Biodiversity Conservation Act 1999 (EPBC Act)**: Treats certain nuclear actions as matters of national environmental significance, creating additional assessment and approval hurdles that function as a practical ban in combination with ARPANS.

**State-level barriers**: Additional prohibitions or restrictions in several states (historically NSW, Queensland, Victoria; some movement on uranium mining bans recently).

**To “delete nuclear regulation” for power generation** (minimum viable set):
- Repeal ARPANS Act s10 (or amend to remove the power plant prohibition).
- Amend EPBC Act nuclear action provisions to remove the effective prohibition while retaining rigorous safety and environmental assessment.
- Harmonise or override conflicting state bans (via Commonwealth power or cooperative federalism).
- Establish (or rapidly expand) a competent nuclear regulatory body with clear, internationally-aligned licensing pathways (drawing on IAEA standards, Canadian, Korean, or US NRC experience).
- Parallel work on waste management framework, liability, and workforce development.

These changes can be legislated relatively quickly (months to 1–2 years if politically prioritised). Building the *competent* regulatory and technical capacity takes longer but can run in parallel with early site selection and technology partnering.

## Fastest Path to Nuclear Power

**Realistic accelerated timeline (aggressive political and institutional commitment)**:

- **Year 0–1**: Legislation passed lifting bans. Nuclear regulator empowered and resourced. Technology partner selected (e.g., proven large PWR from Korea/UAE model, or Western SMR if maturity improves). Site selection on existing industrial/coal sites preferred for grid connection, cooling, and workforce.
- **Years 1–4**: Detailed design, environmental and safety licensing (streamlined but rigorous), early works, supply chain mobilisation, workforce training. First concrete possible by year 4–5 under best-case.
- **Construction**: 6–9 years for first large unit (international median construction times have lengthened; FOAK in a new country adds risk). Parallel units or SMRs can compress later.
- **First power**: Optimistic aggressive case ~2035–2038 for first large reactor. More probable mid-to-late 2030s. Subsequent units faster and cheaper as learning accumulates.

**Cost expectations**:
- International best practice (series-built Korean/UAE-style): roughly A$8,000–12,000/kW or better in mature programs.
- First-of-a-kind Australia: CSIRO GenCost and historical evidence indicate significant premiums (often cited ~100% over international best for FOAK). Large reactor could land in the A$15–25+ billion range for ~1 GW depending on scope, financing, and overruns.
- Advocates using Korean technology transfer claim lower figures (~A$10,000/kW) and 7–11 year total programs are possible with strong delivery focus.
- SMRs currently higher cost per kW in Western projects (e.g., Canadian Darlington benchmarks) but offer potential modularity and factory learning later.
- Financing costs dominate due to long schedules; government-backed offtake, loan guarantees, or direct investment dramatically reduce the risk premium.

**Key accelerators**:
- Technology transfer from proven vendors with recent on-time/on-budget records (Korea, UAE experience).
- Co-location with retiring coal sites (grid, water, workforce, community acceptance).
- Parallel regulatory and construction pathways rather than sequential.
- Early investment in skills (nuclear engineering, welding, project management) leveraging AUKUS naval nuclear expertise.
- Standardised design and fleet approach from day one to capture learning.

**Risks**: Political reversal, regulatory gold-plating, supply-chain bottlenecks, FOAK cost/schedule overruns, public acceptance, waste pathway delays. These are real but manageable with competent execution and clear national priority.

## Implications for AwesomeAustralia Goals

Energy abundance (firm + variable) is the highest-leverage near-term action for per-capita prosperity, housing construction costs, industrial competitiveness, and AI/compute capacity. Nuclear provides high-capacity-factor, low-land-use, weather-independent firm power that complements renewables and reduces the total system cost of high-renewable grids over the long term.

The minimal state core should prioritise energy abundance as a public good. Resource rents from uranium (Australia has large reserves) can help fund the transition and citizen dividends.
